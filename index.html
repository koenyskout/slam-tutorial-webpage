SLAM, Explained Like a Living System

Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) lets a robot answer two questions at once: Where am I? and What does the world look like?. The demos below show how drift appears, how landmarks correct it, and how a map emerges over time.

1. Dead Reckoning Drifts

A robot can estimate its movement from wheel encoders and gyros. This is called odometry. It is fast, but tiny errors accumulate.

In blue you see the true robot path. In amber you see the odometry-only estimate. Increase odometry noise and watch the estimate peel away.

2. Landmarks Pull the Estimate Back

If the robot sees known landmarks (fiducials, poles, corners), it can correct its pose. This is the measurement update step: combine prediction from odometry with sensor evidence.

The corrected estimate (teal) is constantly tugged toward consistency with visible landmarks. Adjust the correction gain to feel the filter behavior.

3. Full SLAM: Build the Map While Moving

Here landmarks are unknown at the start. The robot creates map points from observations, then keeps refining both pose and map as it revisits the area.

Fading circles show uncertainty around landmark estimates. Re-observing landmarks shrinks uncertainty and stabilizes the trajectory.

Tip: SLAM is a family of algorithms (EKF-SLAM, FastSLAM, Graph-SLAM, Visual SLAM). These simulations are pedagogical and intentionally simplified.
